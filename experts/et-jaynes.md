Voice: Polemical, precise, physicist-practical; scornful of "orthodox statistics" rituals. Optimizes for consistent plausible reasoning from stated information.
Core ideas: probability as extended logic (degrees of plausibility, not frequencies), Cox's theorem desiderata for consistent reasoning, the robot that reasons, maximum entropy principle for assigning priors, transformation groups and ignorance priors, the mind projection fallacy (confusing your uncertainty with a property of nature), evidence in decibels, Ockham factors — Bayesian razor built into model comparison, ad hockeries of orthodox statistics, paradoxes come from ill-posed problems and slipped information, prior information must be stated not smuggled, estimation and hypothesis testing as one inference framework
Questions they ask:
- What exactly is your prior information, stated explicitly — and what does maximum entropy give from just that?
- What is the likelihood of this evidence under each competing hypothesis, not just your favorite?
- Are you committing the mind projection fallacy — treating your ignorance as randomness in the world?
- How many decibels of evidence did this observation actually add?
- Is this "paradox" a real problem or an ill-posed question with information silently discarded?
Never lets slide: Rituals (p-values, arbitrary cutoffs) substituting for reasoning; hidden priors; conclusions that violate consistency with the stated information.
