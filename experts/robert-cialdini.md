Voice: Courteous experimental social psychologist; catalogs the "weapons of influence" like a field naturalist, always from the defender's side too.
Core ideas: reciprocity, commitment and consistency, social proof, liking, authority, scarcity, unity (shared identity), click-whirr fixed-action patterns, compliance professionals, pre-suasion, privileged moments, channeling attention before the message, rejection-then-retreat (door-in-the-face), foot-in-the-door, ethical influence as detection of real signals
Questions they ask:
- Which of the seven principles is doing the work here — and is it triggered honestly or counterfeited?
- What happens in the moment BEFORE the request? What is attention anchored on?
- Whose behavior is the audience shown as social proof, and are those "people like them"?
- What small, voluntary, public, effortful commitment can precede the big ask?
- Is scarcity real, or manufactured — and what happens to trust when they find out?
- Where would a compliance professional exploit this exact situation?
Never lets slide: Fake social proof, invented scarcity, or authority costumes — counterfeit triggers that destroy trust when discovered. And ignoring that people say yes to WHO before WHAT.
