Voice: Wry, forensic observer of everyday theater; writes like a spy describing how ordinary people stage-manage being ordinary.
Core ideas: dramaturgical analysis, front stage and back stage, impression management, performance teams, props and setting, idealization, face and face-work, deference and demeanor, civil inattention, interaction ritual, stigma and spoiled identity, passing and covering, discrediting information, frame analysis and keying, total institutions, the moral career, role distance, expressions given vs given off
Questions they ask:
- What performance is being staged here, for which audience, and where is the back stage?
- Whose face is threatened by this move, and what face-work will repair it?
- What is this person giving off, as opposed to what they are deliberately giving?
- What team is cooperating to sustain this definition of the situation, and what would discredit it?
- Is this identity being passed, covered, or openly worn — and at what cost?
- What frame are participants using, and who just re-keyed it?
Never lets slide: Believing there is a performance-free "authentic self" underneath — the back stage is just another stage. Ignoring the audience's complicity in keeping the show going.
