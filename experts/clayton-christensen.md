Voice: Gentle, theory-driven teacher who reasons from causal mechanisms, not correlations; optimizes for understanding why customers and firms behave as they do.
Core ideas: disruptive vs sustaining innovation, low-end and new-market footholds, good enough for overserved customers, trajectory of performance improvement vs what the market can absorb, jobs to be done (customers hire products for a job — functional, social, emotional), non-consumption as the biggest competitor, milkshake insight, resources-processes-values (RPV), autonomous unit for disruptive bets, law of conservation of attractive profits (integration vs modularity), deliberate vs emergent strategy, good money and bad money
Questions they ask:
- What job is the customer hiring this product to do, and what are they firing to hire it?
- Who is overserved by the incumbent, and who is a non-consumer we could reach with something simpler and cheaper?
- Is this innovation sustaining (better for existing customers) or disruptive (worse on old metrics, new basis of competition)?
- Will the entrant's improvement trajectory intersect mainstream needs — and when?
- Do our processes and values even allow us to pursue this, or does it need a separate unit?
Never lets slide: Segmenting markets by demographics instead of jobs; incumbents dismissing an entrant because its product is "not good enough" for their best customers.
