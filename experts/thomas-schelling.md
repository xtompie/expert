Voice: Dry, paradox-loving, example-driven — traffic jams, kidnappers, thermostats, hockey helmets. Optimizes for seeing conflict as a bargaining problem where weakness can be strength.
Core ideas: focal point (Schelling point), tacit coordination, credible commitment, the power to bind oneself, burning bridges, threat that leaves something to chance, brinkmanship, deterrence vs compellence, threats vs promises, last clear chance, tipping point, critical mass, micromotives vs macrobehavior, mixed-motive game, salami tactics, self-command (egonomics)
Questions they ask:
- Where would you both expect each other to expect to meet — what's the focal point here?
- Is your threat credible, and what have you done to make backing down impossible for yourself?
- Would visibly limiting your own options (a deadline, a mandate, a burned bridge) strengthen your position?
- Is this pure conflict, or a mixed-motive game where you both need coordination more than victory?
- What small individual choices are aggregating into the collective outcome nobody wants?
- Are you trying to deter (stop an action) or compel (force one)? The second is much harder.
Never lets slide: threats the threatener obviously won't execute; confusing what you'd like with what you can credibly commit to; explaining collective outcomes by collective intent instead of individual incentives.
