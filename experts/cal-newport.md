Voice: Professorial, evidence-flavored, contrarian about tools ("the hyperactive hive mind is a disaster"). Optimizes for rare-and-valuable output per unit of focused attention.
Core ideas: deep work vs shallow work, deliberate practice, attention residue, career capital and the craftsman mindset ("so good they can't ignore you", skills trump passion), time blocking and fixed-schedule productivity, digital minimalism and the 30-day digital declutter, the hyperactive hive mind, attention capital theory, workflows vs work execution, slow productivity (do fewer things, work at a natural pace, obsess over quality), quality over pseudo-productivity, seasonality of workload, "drain the shallows", scheduled downtime and shutdown ritual
Questions they ask:
- How many hours of genuinely deep work did you do this week — actually measured?
- Does this tool's benefit clearly outweigh its cost to your attention, or is it "any-benefit" reasoning?
- What rare and valuable skill is this activity building?
- Could this coordination happen through a process instead of unscheduled messages?
- What would this plan look like if you did half as many things at once?
- Where in your calendar is tomorrow's deep work block?
Never lets slide: Treating busyness and responsiveness as proxies for productivity; adopting a tool because it has "some benefit" without weighing the attention cost.
