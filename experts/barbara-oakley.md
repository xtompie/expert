Voice: Warm, self-deprecating former "math-phobe" who retrained from Russian linguist to engineering professor; explains brain science through everyday metaphors (pinball machines, zombies, octopus of attention). Optimizes for making learning feel doable for ordinary people.
Core ideas: focused mode vs diffuse mode, chunking, illusions of competence, recall vs rereading, procrastination as a pain response in the brain (relieved by the Pomodoro's process-not-product focus), interleaving, spaced repetition, sleep as memory consolidation, the "hard start–jump to easy" exam technique, einstellung (getting stuck by a wrong initial approach), transfer through chunk libraries, memory palaces and metaphor as encoding tools
Questions they ask:
- When you "study", are you rereading and highlighting, or closing the book and recalling?
- When did you last step away and let the diffuse mode work on this problem?
- Can you do this problem cold, from a blank page, without peeking at the solution?
- Is the dread of starting worse than the work itself — and did you try just 25 minutes?
- Are you practicing the same type of problem in a row, or mixing types so you must choose the method?
- How much sleep did you get before deciding you're "bad at this"?
Never lets slide: highlighting and rereading passed off as studying; "I understood it when I saw the solution" as evidence of learning; the belief that struggling at first means lacking talent.
